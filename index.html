Research Interests

As a Masters student, I have paid particular attention to Natural Language Processing and Machine Learning technologies, particularly with regards to the societal impacts of these technologies. My research projects have focused on ways in which we quantify potential harms of language and ML systems, and the degree to which these measurements accurately capture real-world outcomes. I have worked extensively with word embeddings and techniques for knowledge representation.

Research Projects

  • Intersections of bias in word embeddings
    (Machine Learning. December 2021.)

    Natural language processing algorithms have made tremendous strides in their ability to learn semantic and syntactic patterns from a corpus of documents. Unfortunately, a part of the patterns that machines learn is the underlying bias contained in the training corpus. Word embeddings are one such NLP tool that is susceptible to stereotypes and prejudice. In fact, it has been shown that word embeddings not only capture, but amplify, existing biases in training documents. When we consider that word embeddings contribute to many fields such as translation, hiring, and medical algorithms, it is clear that these biases can cause harm, especially to people who are part of marginalized groups. How can we measure and remove bias from training data? How does identifying intersectional bias differ from bias towards only one group? What can the eliminated documents in training corpora teach us about intersectional bias overall?

  • Measuring the effects of word embedding stereptypes in downstream applications
    (Computational Linguistics. December 2022.)

    Word embeddings allow tokens to be represented in a numeric format, that can be used by machine learning applications to find patterns with other related words, even those which might not have been present in an applications training data. Some computational linguists have even claimed that they are a projection of tokens into a semantic space. Word embeddings have also been shown to derive stereotypical biases from corpora, such as grouping scientific words much closer to masculine-coded words than feminine-coded words. These biases have been studied extensively as phenomena contained within word embeddings, but little research has been done to directly connect these findings to outcomes in machine learning applications. I analyzed several resume classifying models trained on three different corpora, comparing their performance on gender-coded and non-gender coded resumes. I found that the presence of gendered language did distort the results, but often subtly and not always in the direction of the studied phenomenon.

  • Knowledge-based solutions to text-based worlds
    (Artificial Intelligence. May 2022.)

    Text-based games, also called text adventures or interactive fiction, are a form of games meant to evoke reading a work of fiction, where the player controls the main character, popularized in the 1980s by games like Zork I. Players interact with the environment through text commands and the game gives feedback in the form of natural language descriptions. I developed the beginnings of a knowledge and logic-based agent to leverage logical reasoning in solving rudimentary text-based games.

  • turnovertools
    Database, visual effects and video tools for Assistant Film Editors. 2020-2021.

    I have created a number of tools for my work as an Assistant Editor. This suite collects several of the most advanced tools, which may be of interest to other editors. Tools have been created for interfacing Avid Media Composer edits with a FileMaker Pro database for tracking visual effects shots, detecting changes in visual effects shots, generating SQLite3 databases of VFX shots and Avid MediaFiles, and creating reference videos with timecode and reference burns pulled from a visual effects database.
